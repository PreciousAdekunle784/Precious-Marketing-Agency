They arrive on your homepage. Your homepage has a navigation with eight options. It has a hero banner promoting three different things. It has a news section nobody reads. It has a footer with 40 links. It has been designed for every possible visitor — which means it converts almost none of them.

The problem is not your offer. The problem is not your audience. The problem is the page your audience lands on after they click, and the fact that it was not built for them.

The PMA Landing Page Architecture

Every Element on Every
Page Is There for
One Reason. Conversion.

There is a specific sequence of information that moves a visitor from "I just clicked an ad" to "I am ready to buy or enquire." We call it the PMA Landing Page Architecture. Every page we build follows it. Every element within it has a specific job. Click each section to understand what it does and why it matters.

Page Sections
Section 1 of 6
The Hook
The first thing the visitor sees. It has one job: make them stay. The headline must match what the ad said. The subheadline must answer "is this for someone like me?" The hero image or video must reinforce the offer — not decorate the page. Every element above the fold exists to make the visitor scroll one more time.
✓ What We Do
✓Headline mirrors the exact ad copy or search intent that brought the visitor
✓One primary CTA above the fold, high contrast, benefit-led
✓Trust indicators (rating, clients, guarantee) below the headline
✕ What Most Pages Do
✕Generic brand headline with zero connection to the visitor's search
✕Navigation bar with 6–8 escape routes away from the conversion
✕Hero image chosen for aesthetics, no relevance to the offer
3–5 sec
Time to decide stay or leave
+40%
CVR lift from message match alone
Section 2 of 6
The Credibility Bar
Immediately below the fold: the proof that you are real and trustworthy. The visitor is already asking "but how do I know these people are legitimate?" Client logos, review scores, number of clients served, years in business. This section does not sell — it removes the risk of continuing to read.
✓ What We Do
✓Specific numbers: "500+ clients, $84M revenue generated, 4.9★ average rating"
✓Logos of recognisable clients or platforms
✓One-line trust statement matched to the visitor's biggest fear
✕ What Most Pages Do
✕Skip this section entirely and jump straight to product features
✕Vague claims: "industry-leading" and "trusted by businesses"
✕No numbers, no logos, no specifics that the visitor can verify
+28%
CVR lift from specific trust signals vs generic claims
Section 3 of 6
Problem & Agitation
The visitor stays when they feel understood. Name their problem exactly. Use the words they use internally when thinking about it. Then agitate: show them what happens if the problem is not solved. This section converts sceptics into interested prospects by making them feel that the page was written specifically for them.
✓ What We Do
✓Describe the visitor's pain in language they would use themselves
✓Quantify the cost of the problem: time, money, opportunity lost
✓Make them feel that their situation is understood before presenting the solution
✕ What Most Pages Do
✕Skip straight to features and benefits without establishing pain
✕Use generic problem statements that apply to everyone and resonate with no one
✕Talk about themselves instead of the visitor's situation
Section 4 of 6
Offer & Solution
Now — and only now — you present the solution. Specifically. Not "we offer marketing services." The exact mechanism by which you solve the exact problem. Features translated into outcomes. What the visitor's life or business looks like after working with you. This section sells the result, not the service.
✓ What We Do
✓Outcome-led: "you get X result" not "we provide Y service"
✓Specific deliverables, timelines, and measurable outcomes
✓One clear offer — not three packages, not five tiers
✕ What Most Pages Do
✕Lead with features and technical specifications, not outcomes
✕Present 3–5 pricing tiers that create decision paralysis
✕Use jargon the visitor does not understand about a problem they can feel
Section 5 of 6
Social Proof
The visitor is now interested. They are also still sceptical. Social proof at this stage is not decoration — it is the specific evidence that people like them have done what you are asking them to do and experienced the result you are promising. The right proof in the right place is the difference between "I'm interested" and "I'm ready."
✓ What We Do
✓Testimonials with specific results, not vague praise: "went from 2 leads to 47 per month"
✓Industry-relevant social proof matched to the visitor's niche
✓Before/after numbers, not just sentiment
✕ What Most Pages Do
✕"Great company to work with!" — no specifics, no proof of outcome
✕Generic 5-star reviews with no industry context
✕Case studies buried on a separate page rather than placed at the decision point
Section 6 of 6
CTA & Risk Removal
The only section that actually produces conversions. A CTA is not a button. It is the answer to the question "what happens if I do this?" The button text must state the outcome, not the action. The risk removal directly addresses the one remaining objection: "what do I lose if this does not work out?" Zero-risk framing converts prospects into buyers.
✓ What We Do
✓Button says what you get: "Get My Free Audit" not "Submit"
✓Risk removal directly below button: "Free. No obligation. Takes 60 seconds."
✓Minimal form fields — only what is needed to take the next step
✕ What Most Pages Do
✕"Submit" or "Click Here" — describes the action, not the reward
✕9-field form before they have decided they want to proceed
✕No risk removal — visitor still carrying all the perceived risk of engaging
+325%
CVR: 9-field form → 3-field form
+121%
CVR: "Submit" → benefit-led CTA
